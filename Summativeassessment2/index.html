Introduction

Discover The Milky Way

We’re a Bristol-based planetarium, and we love space! We are predominantly an educational organisation, offering educational entertainment for the public and for schools. We want to create a website that explains all the planets in the Milky Way, and we were hoping you could create a prototype for us. We want the site to be accessible to as many people as possible, in the hopes that anyone can learn more about outer space and get excited like we do. It is important to us that our materials be fun, engaging, and interesting – but for as many people as possible. We don’t want anyone to feel as though science and other complicated topics “aren’t for them”.

But how can we know about other parts of the Milky Way if we don’t even know our home? We need to cover up our Solar System, our Planets, and our Home. Think of the Milky Way as a giant city. If you want to explore the whole city, you first need to know your own house and street. Our Solar System is that "home street." By learning how far apart the planets are from our Sun, we get a better feeling for how huge space actually is before we try to understand the entire galaxy.

Also, our Solar System works like a mini-version of the galaxy. The same gravity that keeps Earth spinning around the Sun is what keeps billions of other stars spinning around the center of the Milky Way. Once you get how our little neighborhood was born from dust and gas, you’ll understand the "blueprint" for how the whole massive galaxy was built!

About our Home

The Solar System

Our solar system is like our tiny home address in a massive city of billions of stars. It consists of our star, the Sun, and everything bound to it by gravity—the planets Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune, along with dwarf planets like Pluto, dozens of moons, and millions of asteroids and comets. Think of the solar system as our "home street"; by learning how far apart our eight planets are from the Sun, we get a better feeling for how huge space actually is before we try to understand the entire Milky Way.

The order and arrangement of the planets and other bodies in our solar system is due to the way the solar system formed. Nearest the Sun, only rocky material could withstand the heat when the solar system was young. For this reason, the first four planets— Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are terrestrial planets. They're small with solid, rocky surfaces. Meanwhile, materials we are used to seeing as ice, liquid or gas settled in the outer regions of the young solar system. Gravity pulled these materials together, and that is where we find gas giants Jupiter and Saturn and ice giants Uranus and Neptune.

Planets in our System

Descriptions of Planets in our Solar System

Our Solar System is a massive neighborhood floating in deep space, held together by the Sun’s powerful gravity. It features eight main planets divided into two groups: the four rocky inner worlds and the four giant outer worlds. Understanding this local area is important because it acts as a cosmic "yardstick," helping us sense how huge the universe really is.

The inner planets— Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are solid worlds you could actually walk on. Mercury is a tiny speedster with the shortest year, while Venus is a scorching oven trapped under thick clouds. Earth is our perfect "Goldilocks" home with liquid water, and Mars is a red desert featuring a volcano three times taller than Mount Everest.

Past the asteroid belt, you find the giants. Jupiter and Saturn are "Gas Giants" made of hydrogen and helium. Jupiter is famous for its massive storms, while Saturn is known for its spectacular rings made of ice and rock. Further out are the "Ice Giants," Uranus and Neptune. Uranus is the weirdo that spins on its side, and Neptune is a deep blue world with winds faster than a jet plane.

By studying these worlds, we learn the "blueprint" for how gravity and chemistry work together. Every planet, from a small rock to a massive gas ball, tells a story about the forces that build everything we see in space.

Size and Distance

Size and Distance on all Planets in the Solar System

Our solar system extends much farther than the eight planets that orbit the Sun. The solar system also includes the Kuiper Belt that lies past Neptune's orbit. This is a sparsely occupied ring of icy bodies, almost all smaller than the most popular Kuiper Belt Object, dwarf planet Pluto.

And beyond the fringes of the Kuiper belt is the Oort Cloud. This giant spherical shell surrounds our solar system. It has never been directly observed, but its existence is predicted based on mathematical models and observations of comets that likely originate there.

The Oort Cloud is made of icy pieces of space debris the sizes of mountains and sometimes larger, orbiting our Sun as far as 1.6 light years away. This shell of material is thick, extending from 5,000 astronomical units to 100,000 astronomical units. One astronomical unit (or AU) is the distance from the Sun to Earth, or about 93 million miles (150 million kilometers). The Oort Cloud is the boundary of the Sun's gravitational influence, where orbiting objects can turn around and return closer to our Sun.

The Sun's heliosphere doesn't extend quite as far. The heliosphere is the bubble created by the solar wind—a stream of electrically charged gas blowing outward from the Sun in all directions. The boundary where the solar wind is abruptly slowed by pressure from interstellar gases is called the termination shock. This edge occurs between 80-100 astronomical units.

Two NASA spacecraft, launched in 1977, have crossed the termination shock: Voyager 1 in 2004 and Voyager 2 in 2007. But it will be many thousands of years before the two Voyagers exit the Oort Cloud.

Moons

All known Moons in the Solar System

The moons in our neighborhood are spread out very unevenly, with the giant outer planets holding almost all of them. Saturn is the current champion with 146 moons, including the massive, cloud-covered Titan. Jupiter follows closely with 95 moons, featuring the four famous "Galilean" moons that are large enough to be seen with basic binoculars. The ice giants have fewer, with Uranus holding 28 and Neptune claiming 16, including Triton, which orbits the planet backward.

In the inner solar system, moons are much rarer. Mars has two tiny, potato-shaped moons called Phobos and Deimos, while Earth has just our one familiar Moon that controls our tides. Mercury and Venus are the only ones with no moons at all, likely because they are so close to the Sun that its gravity would have snatched any moons away. Together, these hundreds of moons range from giant, ocean-filled worlds to small, lumpy rocks.

Potential for Life

The chance of us living on different Planet or the chance of Alien Lifeforms

  1. Earth: 100% - The only place we know for sure has life. It has the perfect mix of air, water, and heat.
  2. Mars: Possible - Scientists found evidence of ancient rivers. Tiny life might still be hiding in ice or deep underground.
  3. Venus: Unlikely but possible - The surface is way too hot, but tiny microbes could potentially live high up in the cooler clouds.
  4. Jupiter & Saturn: Moon Dependent - The gas giants themselves are too harsh, but their moons (like Europa and Enceladus) have warm, salty oceans hidden under ice.
  5. Uranus & Neptune: Remote Chance - These are mostly frozen, but the intense pressure deep inside might create pockets of warm water where life could survive.
